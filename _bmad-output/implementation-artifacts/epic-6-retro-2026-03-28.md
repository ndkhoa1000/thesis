## Context

- Epic 6 is fully complete: Stories 6.1 through 6.5 are `done`, and this retrospective closes the epic formally in sprint tracking.
- Epic 6 delivered the first attendant- and operator-facing operational accountability layer on top of the core parking loop: occupancy visibility, announcements, timeout correction, shift handover, and final day-end close-out.
- The epic depended heavily on Epic 4's atomic session/payment boundaries and Epic 5's authoritative availability/event patterns, so architectural discipline mattered more than feature count.
- Previous retrospective commitments from Epic 5 were used as the follow-through baseline.

## Part 1: Epic Review

### What Went Well

- Epic 6 delivered a coherent operational-management slice instead of isolated admin utilities. Attendant occupancy, stale-session repair, shift accountability, and operator alert handling now support each other as one workflow family.
- Backend ownership of operational truth remained strong throughout the epic. Occupancy, timeout transitions, shift cash reconciliation, discrepancy handling, and final close-out state all stayed server-derived rather than being approximated on the client.
- The team reused existing seams effectively. `sessions.py`, `shifts.py`, and the attendant/operator feature modules absorbed new behavior without forcing a framework reset or speculative module explosion.
- Review quality materially improved the epic. The most important hardening came from review findings around authorization leakage, visibility gaps, lifecycle correctness, and the distinction between handover and final shift close-out.
- The team showed good scope discipline despite expanding operational complexity. Announcements stayed lot-scoped and in-app, timeout stayed a narrow corrective flow rather than becoming a generic session editor, and shift accountability reused the existing notification domain instead of expanding into Epic 10 territory.
- Validation standards were high by the end of the epic. Stories closed with backend Docker validation plus Flutter regression runs, and the Story 6.5 follow-up finished with full backend and full Flutter suites green.

### What Did Not Go Well

- Story readiness was uneven across the epic. Stories 6.1 to 6.3 fit existing system seams well, but Story 6.4 introduced a new accountability domain that was not fully modeled up front, which led directly to Story 6.5 being created as a follow-up to correct the lifecycle boundary.
- The attendant operational feature area became denser quickly. Occupancy, active-session management, timeout resolution, handover, and final close-out now coexist in one workspace, increasing future UX and regression pressure even though the current result is still workable.
- Several high-value defects were not implementation bugs in the narrow sense; they were boundary-definition issues discovered during review. The strongest example was the mistaken assumption that the final shift of day could share the same mechanics as attendant-to-attendant handover.
- Backend unit tests showed fragility around mocked query ordering. When Story 6.5 added the pending-close-out guard, legacy tests failed because they modeled old `db.execute` call counts too tightly.
- Epic closure hygiene again needed an explicit final pass. The code was green before the retrospective/tracker state was synchronized, which means process closure still lags behind implementation closure unless it is handled deliberately.

### Follow-Through From Epic 5 Retrospective

- Close epic-tracking hygiene in the same pass as epic completion: improved but still not automatic. Epic 6 is now fully synchronized, but only after an explicit closeout pass rather than by default workflow behavior.
- Treat Story 5.4 availability event as the shared occupancy contract for Epic 6: completed. Story 6.1 reused authoritative availability semantics, and Story 6.3 preserved the same post-mutation publication and occupancy-truth discipline.
- Keep demo seed data and smoke scenarios aligned with user-facing flows: partially improved. Test and verification discipline was strong, but test-generated artifacts still needed cleanup during implementation.
- Preserve driver discovery modularity as new features land: completed where relevant. Epic 6 largely respected feature boundaries and did not collapse new operational behavior back into unrelated bootstrap surfaces.
- Run a design-first preparation pass for Story 6.4 before implementation: partially completed. The team did create a strong implementation artifact and review loop, but the eventual Story 6.5 follow-up shows that the final-shift lifecycle boundary was still under-specified before coding began.

### Recurring Patterns Across Stories

- The strongest stories were the ones that kept Flutter as a thin workflow shell over backend-owned state and mutation rules.
- `sessions.py`, `shift_service.py`, and the attendant workspace became the epic's highest-density change surfaces. Reuse was efficient, but those files now carry real maintenance risk.
- Review consistently added the most value when it challenged lifecycle correctness, authorization scope, and mutation boundaries, not just UI existence.
- Honest blocking behavior improved trust. Missing capacity, hidden announcements, duplicate timeout transitions, discrepancy rationale, and lot-not-empty close-out all became explicit product states instead of silent fallbacks.
- Follow-up work was healthy when it fixed the model rather than patching symptoms. Story 6.5 is a good example: the team created a separate close-out lifecycle instead of deforming `ShiftHandover` to fit a new business case.

### Root Lessons Learned

1. Operational accountability features must model business lifecycles explicitly; superficially similar workflows can still require separate persistence and API seams.
2. Backend-owned state remains the main quality anchor for occupancy, payments, and shift trust flows.
3. Review is most valuable when it attacks boundary assumptions, not only correctness of the implemented happy path.
4. Mock-heavy unit tests should avoid overfitting exact internal query sequencing when policy guards are likely to evolve.
5. Epic completion should include tracker, retrospective, and artifact synchronization as part of the definition of done, not as an afterthought.

## Part 2: Next Epic Preparation

### Next Epic Readiness

- Epic 7 can begin from a stable operational base. The system now has authoritative occupancy views, attendant corrective tools, in-app announcement visibility, shift accountability primitives, and explicit day-end close-out handling.
- No strategic rewrite is needed before Epic 7. Booking can build on the existing capacity, session, and lot-availability contracts rather than introducing parallel truth models.
- The main readiness risk is complexity concentration. Future work should be careful not to overload the attendant workspace or central backend route modules without extracting sharper seams when needed.

### Critical Discoveries That Affect Upcoming Epics

1. Booking and pre-book conversion in Epic 7 should reuse the same authoritative capacity and session-mutation boundaries rather than inventing booking-specific availability shortcuts.
2. Shift and close-out domain concepts introduced in Epic 6 are now real system primitives and should be treated as reusable accountability infrastructure, not one-off story code.
3. Operator-facing alerts worked well as an in-app domain extension; future notification-heavy work should still defer push complexity until Epic 10 unless product scope explicitly changes.
4. The attendant workspace is now a true operational console. Additional features should be added carefully to avoid mixing high-speed gate actions with slower administrative workflows.

### Team Agreements For Future Epics

- Keep backend ownership of occupancy, payment, shift, and booking truth whenever user actions depend on fresh operational state.
- Use review to challenge lifecycle boundaries, authorization scope, and mutation lock semantics rather than only verifying visible behavior.
- Treat explicit blocked/error states as part of product quality when safety or financial integrity is involved.
- Consider epic closure incomplete until code, regressions, tracker state, and retrospective artifacts all agree.

### Preparation Recommendations For Epic 7 and Beyond

- Start Epic 7 by reusing the existing capacity/session invariants rather than introducing booking-side shortcuts.
- Keep new booking logic separate from high-friction shift/accountability flows so the attendant experience does not become cognitively overloaded.
- Watch `sessions.py`, `shifts.py`, and the attendant workspace for density; extract seams when the next story would otherwise pile unrelated concerns into the same module.
- Continue preferring follow-up work that corrects the business model cleanly over patches that preserve a flawed lifecycle abstraction.

## Summary

Epic 6 successfully turned the system from a working parking loop into a more credible operations product with attendant visibility, corrective authority, and shift-level accountability. The biggest success was not just feature delivery, but the team's willingness to preserve backend truth and fix lifecycle mistakes at the model boundary when review exposed them. The clearest lesson is that operational and financial workflows demand more than correct code paths; they require explicit lifecycle modeling, strong blocking states, and disciplined closure hygiene. Epic 7 is ready to proceed, but it should build on Epic 6's authoritative state patterns while keeping an eye on growing module and workspace density.
