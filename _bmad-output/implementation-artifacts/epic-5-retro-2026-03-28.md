## Context

- Epic 5 is now fully complete: Stories 5.1 through 5.4 are `done`, and this retrospective closes the epic formally in sprint tracking.
- Epic 5 replaced the mock driver discovery experience with backend-backed map discovery, lot details, parking history, and an in-app en-route fullness advisory.
- The epic built directly on Epic 4's authoritative session and payment lifecycle, so the quality of server-owned data contracts mattered more than UI polish alone.
- Previous retrospective commitments from Epic 4 were used as the follow-through baseline.

## Part 1: Epic Review

### What Went Well

- Epic 5 delivered a coherent driver-facing product slice instead of disconnected screens. Map discovery, lot details, completed-session history, and the en-route advisory now form a credible end-to-end parking-discovery experience.
- Backend ownership of business truth stayed strong across all stories. Availability remained anchored on `ParkingLot.current_available`, lot trends were server-derived with honest empty states, driver history came from completed session plus payment records, and Story 5.4 introduced a canonical realtime availability event instead of client polling.
- The team improved structural hygiene in the mobile app. Extracting the mock map flow out of `mobile/lib/main.dart` into dedicated feature modules created a usable seam for later stories instead of compounding bootstrap-file debt.
- Scope discipline was consistently good. The team resisted turning Story 5.2 into speculative analytics, Story 5.4 into push notifications, or Epic 5 generally into booking/navigation automation.
- Review hardening meaningfully improved outcomes. The review pass caught future-dated lot-detail pricing exposure, map-tap reachability gaps, chart truncation, and several UX/state issues before the epic closed.
- Validation quality was stronger than in earlier epics. Story 5 work closed with targeted backend and Flutter tests, and several stories also reran broader regression suites rather than relying only on local happy-path checks.

### What Did Not Go Well

- Epic closure hygiene lagged behind implementation. By the time all four stories were done, sprint tracking still showed `epic-5: in-progress`, which is a process gap rather than a product gap.
- The driver discovery surface accumulated significant responsibility quickly. Map rendering, permission fallback, quick-card actions, detail entry, history entry, realtime watch state, and advisory messaging now live close together, which increases future regression risk even though the feature is still manageable.
- The realtime seam was architecturally expected but not pre-built. Story 5.4 had to establish the actual event hub and authenticated websocket contract during feature delivery, which added platform work late in the epic.
- Several of the most important defects were not obvious on first implementation. They appeared during review or closeout as contract-freshness, UI reachability, layout, and seed-data consistency problems.
- Documentation and demo-environment alignment required explicit cleanup at the end. Manual QA docs, seeded accounts, and operator demo states were accurate only after a dedicated closeout pass.

### Follow-Through From Epic 4 Retrospective

- Clear Stories 4.5 and 4.6 through review and move Epic 4 to actual closure: completed. Epic 4 is now closed in sprint tracking, and Epic 5 was able to rely on the resulting stable checkout and payment lifecycle.
- Treat settlement concurrency as a standard review lens: partially carried forward. Epic 5 did not center on financial concurrency, but it did preserve post-commit publication discipline for availability changes and kept server-owned mutation boundaries intact.
- Keep future exit media on the same backend contract family: preserved. Epic 5 did not reopen checkout-boundary ambiguity or introduce side-channel session mutations.
- Tighten generated-file hygiene for local media-backed tests and demos: completed. The stray artifact issue identified in Epic 4 was explicitly cleaned up during Epic 5 work.
- Preserve the QR-first MVP scope rule until product explicitly expands exit media: completed. Epic 5 stayed within the intended discovery/history scope and did not blur into unplanned exit-media work.

### Recurring Patterns Across Stories

- The most stable stories were the ones that treated Flutter as a thin client over backend-owned read models and event contracts.
- `lots.py`, `sessions.py`, and the driver discovery feature became the epic's main extension points. Reuse was efficient, but those modules are now high-density change surfaces.
- Review consistently added more value when it challenged freshness, boundary ownership, and real user reachability instead of only checking that the happy path existed.
- The team got better at honest degradation. Permission-denied fallback, insufficient-data trend states, and advisory-only full-lot alerts all preserved user trust without pretending the product knew more than it did.
- Closeout quality depended on more than code. Seed scripts, manual QA docs, and story-status artifacts were all part of whether the epic was actually ready to hand off.

### Root Lessons Learned

1. Discovery features stay trustworthy only when availability, pricing, trends, and history all come from a single server-owned source of truth.
2. Extracting feature boundaries early is cheaper than carrying prototype UI structure through multiple stories.
3. A canonical realtime contract is product infrastructure, not just a one-story implementation detail.
4. Honest fallback and empty-state behavior is a quality feature, not a scope compromise.
5. Epic closure should include tracker, test/demo data, and manual QA synchronization in the same pass as the final story completion.

## Part 2: Next Epic Preparation

### Next Epic Readiness

- Epic 6 can begin from a solid base. The system now has authoritative occupancy-affecting session flows from Epic 4, driver-visible lot discovery and detail surfaces from Epic 5, and a canonical realtime availability stream that future occupancy views can reuse.
- No fundamental rewrite of Epic 6 is required. The planned direction still fits the current architecture and product boundaries.
- The main readiness risk is uneven implementation maturity across upcoming stories. Stories 6.1 through 6.3 have clear seams in the current system, while Story 6.4 introduces shift-transfer concepts that are not yet scaffolded in code.

### Critical Discoveries That Affect Upcoming Epics

1. Story 6.1 should reuse the existing availability and capacity semantics instead of inventing a second occupancy-calculation path for attendants.
2. Story 6.2 can build on the driver lot-detail surface introduced in Story 5.2, but it needs an operator authoring flow plus visibility-window rules that remain backend-owned.
3. Story 6.3 must preserve the same authoritative session-close and availability-mutation boundaries established in Epic 4 and extended by Epic 5 realtime publication.
4. Story 6.4 is the least prepared story technically. Shift state, cash reconciliation, discrepancy capture, and QR handoff semantics need an explicit design pass before implementation begins.

### Team Agreements For Future Epics

- Keep backend ownership of availability, pricing, history, and session truth whenever user decisions depend on fresh operational state.
- Use review to challenge data freshness, workflow reachability, and post-commit publication semantics, not just feature existence.
- Treat story closure as incomplete until code, tracker state, seed/demo data, and manual QA documentation all agree.

### Preparation Recommendations For Epic 6 and Beyond

- Start Epic 6 with Story 6.1 or Story 6.2, where the current seams are clearest and reuse value is highest.
- Do not treat Story 6.4 as ordinary CRUD work; it is a workflow-and-accountability feature with new domain concepts and should be prepared accordingly.
- Reuse the current realtime availability contract wherever occupancy changes are surfaced to attendants or drivers unless the contract proves insufficient.
- Continue keeping advanced notification behavior out of scope until the dedicated notification epic, even if Epic 6 introduces more driver-visible announcements.

## Summary

Epic 5 successfully transformed the driver experience from a mock proof-of-concept into a backend-backed discovery product with credible realtime behavior and completed-session history. The strongest success was architectural discipline: the team kept business truth on the server, extracted mobile feature boundaries before they collapsed into the bootstrap shell, and used review to harden reachability and data-freshness issues. The main lesson is that discovery quality depends as much on canonical contracts, demo-environment truth, and artifact hygiene as it does on visible UI delivery. Epic 6 does not need a strategic reset, but it should deliberately reuse Epic 5's availability foundations and give Story 6.4 upfront design attention before implementation.
