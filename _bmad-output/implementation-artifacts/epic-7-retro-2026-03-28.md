## Context

- Epic 7 is fully complete: Stories 7.1 through 7.3 are `done`, and this retrospective closes the epic formally in sprint tracking.
- Epic 7 delivered the first complete booking lifecycle in the product: driver-side reservation and deposit capture, attendant-side booking conversion at the gate, and backend-managed no-show expiration.
- The epic depended directly on Epic 4's session mutation boundaries, Epic 5's authoritative availability publication, and Epic 6's guidance to reuse existing operational seams instead of inventing booking-specific shortcuts.
- Previous retrospective commitments from Epic 6 were used as the follow-through baseline.

## Part 1: Epic Review

### What Went Well

- Epic 7 preserved backend ownership of booking truth from end to end. Booking creation, payment linkage, gate conversion, expiration, and availability release all remained server-derived instead of being approximated in Flutter.
- The team reused the right seams instead of branching the architecture. `bookings.py`, `sessions.py`, the existing QR token pattern, and the canonical lot-availability publication path absorbed the new behavior without a speculative reservation subsystem.
- Driver and attendant UX stayed disciplined. Story 7.1 extended the lot-details flow instead of creating a disconnected booking funnel, and Story 7.2 kept the attendant experience scanner-first instead of introducing a second booking-only gate surface.
- Lifecycle integrity improved as the epic progressed. Explicit consumed and expired handling, conversion-time revalidation, and exact-once capacity restoration made the booking flow materially safer than a thin happy-path implementation would have been.
- Validation quality remained high. Story closure notes consistently referenced backend Docker pytest runs, local `flutter test`, and focused hardening passes after review findings.
- Epic 7 turned prior retrospective guidance into implementation behavior. Booking reused existing capacity/session invariants and authoritative availability contracts rather than creating parallel state models.

### What Did Not Go Well

- Booking looked simpler in planning than it was in execution. The core user journey was straightforward, but the real complexity lived in lifecycle edges such as stale active-booking reads, expired token conversion, and rebooking against stale client state.
- High-density backend and UI surfaces became denser again. `bookings.py`, `sessions.py`, lot-details booking UI, and the attendant scanner now carry more cross-story responsibility and are becoming real maintenance hotspots.
- Several important fixes were discovered during review follow-up rather than in the first implementation pass. The epic still shipped solid outcomes, but the final quality bar depended on explicit hardening after initial completion.
- Cross-role workflows increased regression pressure. Driver booking, attendant conversion, expiration worker logic, and availability publication all touch the same operational truth, so a change in one part of the flow can disturb multiple actor experiences.
- Epic closure hygiene still required a deliberate final pass. The story artifacts were complete before the retrospective and tracker state were synchronized, which means the process definition of done is still not self-executing.

### Follow-Through From Epic 6 Retrospective

- Build tracker and retrospective closure into the standard epic closeout workflow: partially improved. Epic 7 implementation artifacts were strong, but tracker closure still waited for this retrospective pass instead of happening automatically with story completion.
- Reduce mock-sequence brittleness in backend mutation tests: partially evidenced. Backend regressions stayed green through multiple follow-up hardening passes, but the story records do not yet show a broad test-design cleanup at the same level as the Epic 6 recommendation.
- Protect the attendant workspace from operational overload: completed. Story 7.2 extended the existing scanner-first workflow without splitting attention across a second booking scanner or pushing booking management into the gate workspace.
- Treat new business lifecycle assumptions as review targets before implementation deepens: completed. The epic's most valuable hardening focused exactly on lifecycle boundaries such as active vs. consumed vs. expired booking state and stale-token handling.
- Reuse Epic 6 accountability seams in future operational features instead of duplicating logic: completed. Booking, conversion, expiration, and availability publication all built on the existing operational state model rather than bypassing it.

### Recurring Patterns Across Stories

- The strongest implementation decisions kept Flutter thin and pushed booking, payment, expiration, and capacity truth to the backend.
- Review added the most value when it challenged lifecycle boundaries and stale-state assumptions, not when it only verified visible feature presence.
- Canonical availability publication remained a reliable system seam. Booking create, cancel, conversion, and expiration all benefited from treating availability updates as one authoritative backend contract.
- The epic repeatedly rewarded explicit terminal-state modeling. Consumed, cancelled, and expired bookings became safer once they were treated as distinct states with separate read and mutation behavior.
- Story follow-up work was healthy when it corrected the model instead of patching symptoms. The review-closeout notes consistently strengthened the contract at the state boundary rather than hiding issues in the UI.

### Root Lessons Learned

1. Reservation features are not mainly UI problems; they are lifecycle-integrity problems that need explicit state modeling and revalidation at every mutation boundary.
2. Backend-owned operational truth remains the main quality anchor for booking, payment, and capacity correctness.
3. Review is most valuable when it attacks stale-state behavior, token reuse, and terminal-state semantics before those assumptions spread across more stories.
4. Reusing existing operational seams is the right strategy, but dense surfaces such as `bookings.py` and `sessions.py` now need closer watch for maintainability.
5. Epic completion still is not truly done until code, regressions, tracker state, and retrospective artifacts are synchronized in one closeout pass.

## Part 2: Next Epic Preparation

### Next Epic Readiness

- Epic 8 can proceed without a strategic rewrite. Epic 7 leaves the platform with a stable booking lifecycle, explicit terminal states, and trustworthy payment/capacity data that business-management features can build on.
- No major planning reversal is required for Epic 8. The lease-contract and revenue-dashboard scope still makes sense, and Epic 7 does not invalidate the current direction.
- The main readiness risk is not architecture failure; it is source-of-truth drift. Revenue and contract features must read from canonical lease, payment, and session state instead of creating dashboard-only interpretations that diverge from operational truth.

### Critical Discoveries That Affect Upcoming Epics

1. Revenue reporting should build directly on canonical `Payment`, `ParkingSession`, and approved lease state rather than on client-maintained aggregates or duplicated finance models.
2. The platform now has multiple stateful backend-issued artifacts in flight: driver QR tokens, booking confirmation, and expiration state. Future owner/operator workflows should preserve the same backend-trusted contract pattern whenever approvals or documents matter.
3. Epic 8 should replace the temporary lease-bootstrap bridge cleanly, not layer another shortcut on top of it. The planning note added after earlier retrospectives remains valid.
4. High-speed operational UX and slower business-management UX should stay separated. Epic 8 should avoid leaking lease or dashboard concerns into the attendant and booking surfaces that need fast, low-friction interactions.

### Team Agreements For Future Epics

- Treat lifecycle-state review as a first-class quality gate whenever a story introduces long-lived backend state or tokenized workflows.
- Keep availability, payment, contract, and revenue truth backend-owned when user trust depends on fresh authoritative state.
- Prefer extending existing surfaces only when that preserves workflow clarity; extract a new seam when one more feature would overload a high-density module or screen.
- Consider epic closure incomplete until code, regressions, tracker state, and retrospective artifacts all agree.

### Preparation Recommendations For Epic 8 and Beyond

- Use Story 8.1 to replace the temporary lease-bootstrap path with the proper contract lifecycle and approval flow.
- Build owner and operator dashboards on canonical financial and session records rather than dashboard-specific calculations hidden in the client.
- Keep business-management workflows separated from attendant and booking operational flows so the gate experience stays fast and focused.
- Watch `bookings.py`, `sessions.py`, and other central mutation surfaces for density; extract service seams if Epic 8 starts stacking unrelated business logic onto them.

## Summary

Epic 7 succeeded because the team treated booking as an operational-state problem instead of a simple reservation UI feature. The strongest outcome was not only that drivers can now book and attendants can convert those bookings, but that the system preserves trustworthy capacity, payment linkage, and expiration behavior through backend-owned contracts. The main lesson is that stateful lifecycle features need explicit terminal-state modeling, stale-state hardening, and disciplined closeout hygiene. Epic 8 is ready to proceed, provided it builds revenue and lease management on the same canonical truth rather than introducing business-side shortcuts.
