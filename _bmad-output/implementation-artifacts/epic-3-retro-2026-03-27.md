Epic: 3
Project: thesis

## Context

- Epic 3 is complete with Stories 3.1, 3.2, and 3.3 marked `done` in sprint tracking.
- This retrospective covers the first Operator-facing operational slice: lot setup, lot-wide hours and pricing, and attendant account provisioning.
- The retrospective also includes post-completion discoveries surfaced during review and UAT-oriented follow-up, because they materially affect how usable Epic 3 is in the actual app.
- Previous retrospective commitments from Epic 2 were used as the comparison baseline for follow-through.

## Part 1: Epic Review

### What Went Well

- Epic 3 delivered a coherent Operator foundation instead of isolated CRUD fragments. By the end of the epic, an Operator can manage leased lots, configure lot-wide operating rules, and provision dedicated Attendant credentials from one functional workspace.
- Reuse decisions were strong throughout the epic. The team extended the same operator lot-management flow across Stories 3.1, 3.2, and 3.3 instead of spawning parallel shells or temporary data models.
- Domain alignment remained disciplined. Active lot access stayed tied to `LotLease.status == ACTIVE`, lot configuration kept using versioned `ParkingLotConfig`, and pricing reused the existing `Pricing` model with lot-wide `vehicle_type = ALL` semantics.
- The separate-account model for Attendants proved correct. Story 3.3 kept Attendant credentials outside the public multi-capability account flow, which preserved a clean boundary for future Epic 4 gate operations.
- Verification discipline stayed strong. Backend Docker tests and local Flutter widget tests remained the default validation path, and review findings were actually patched instead of documented and deferred.

### What Did Not Go Well

- Epic 3 exposed a growing gap between “feature implemented” and “feature realistically testable in the app.” Operator features depended on active leases and multi-capability navigation, but those supporting flows were not yet present in the user-facing shell.
- The mobile shell debt became operationally expensive. The role-first `if/else` routing in `main.dart` was still tolerable in Epic 2, but in Epic 3 it became a real UAT blocker once one public user could legitimately act as both Lot Owner and Operator.
- Review surfaced a serious lifecycle issue: deactivated accounts could still authenticate and use refresh/access token flows. That undermined Story 3.3 attendant removal semantics and showed that account moderation behavior was not yet truly enforced system-wide.
- Story acceptance was sometimes ahead of surrounding workflow readiness. The operator stories were valid in isolation, but actual end-to-end exercise required either Epic 8 lease management or a bridging path for demo/UAT usage.
- UI interaction reliability still needed late tightening. The attendant-management widget flow required iterative fixes around bottom-sheet behavior, viewport constraints, and action execution.

### Follow-Through From Epic 2 Retrospective

- Verification checklist discipline: improved further. Epic 3 kept the backend Docker plus Flutter widget testing pattern as the working default.
- Workflow-specific filtering and intent-specific contracts: improved. Operator endpoints remained scoped to active leases and lot-specific actions instead of broad management payloads.
- Scope annotation for future-dependent behavior: partially improved. Story notes clearly called out Epic 4 dependencies for enforcement and fee calculation, but UAT readiness still depended on extra supporting flows that were not fully surfaced early enough.
- Shell standardization: still unresolved at the epic level. Epic 3 confirmed the risk flagged in Epic 2; the shell issue is no longer theoretical.
- Review-driven interaction hardening: improved. Several issues were closed during review instead of being ignored, but the fact they appeared repeatedly suggests the pattern is not yet preventive.

### Recurring Patterns Across Stories

- Reusing the existing workspace is effective when the data model is already compatible, but only if the surrounding shell can actually route users into that workspace cleanly.
- The strongest implementation results came from extending stable backend primitives instead of inventing shortcuts. `LotLease`, `ParkingLotConfig`, `Pricing`, and dedicated `ATTENDANT` users all scaled well across the epic.
- Review continued to produce the highest-value findings around lifecycle integrity and workflow reality, not around basic happy-path CRUD behavior.
- “Done” at the story level now needs two checks: whether the feature contract is correct, and whether a real user can actually reach and exercise that contract through the current app.

### Root Lessons Learned

1. Operator workflows cannot be treated as self-contained once the business model depends on leased lots and additive public capabilities. Delivery must account for navigation and workflow reachability, not only endpoint correctness.
2. Deactivation logic is not complete until authentication, bearer access, and token refresh all enforce the same inactive-user rule.
3. A placeholder shell can support early feature delivery, but by Epic 3 it became a system constraint that affected testing, demos, and perceived completeness.
4. Bridging features are acceptable when they are explicit, minimal, and aimed at unblocking validation, but they should be documented as bridges rather than mistaken for the final workflow architecture.
5. Versioned configuration models were a good architectural choice. They let the team expand lot setup behavior across multiple stories without schema churn.

## Part 2: Next Epic Preparation

### Next Epic Readiness

- Epic 4 is now better prepared because Epic 3 established the core lot configuration data and dedicated attendant identities needed for check-in and check-out operations.
- The project is also carrying clearer cross-epic constraints than before. Epic 4 can proceed functionally, but workspace access, shell structure, and lease lifecycle realism are now visible enabling concerns.
- Epic 8 and Epic 9 have become more important preparation anchors than previously assumed. Epic 8 owns the proper lease business flow, and Epic 9 owns the durable shell/navigation solution that Epic 3 proved the app now needs.

### Critical Discoveries That Affect Upcoming Epics

1. Epic 4 can build operational attendant flows on top of Story 3.3, but those flows depend on strong inactive-account enforcement to make attendant removal meaningful.
2. Epic 8 lease lifecycle work is no longer just a business-management enhancement. It directly affects whether Operator workflows are naturally reachable without temporary bootstrap paths.
3. Epic 9 shell work should be treated as validation infrastructure, not cosmetic cleanup. Multi-capability role switching is now a tested requirement, not a future nice-to-have.
4. The current Operator workspace can continue to expand functionally, but each additional feature will be harder to demo and verify if it assumes shell and lease flow gaps will somehow disappear later.

### Team Agreements For Future Epics

- Do not declare management stories effectively complete unless the intended user can actually navigate into the workflow and exercise it.
- Keep bridge solutions small, explicit, and replaceable. If a workaround exists to unblock UAT, it should not silently become the long-term architecture.
- Treat auth lifecycle consistency as platform behavior, not feature-local behavior.
- Continue syncing implementation artifacts, review fixes, and sprint-status updates in the same closure pass.

### Preparation Recommendations For Epic 4 and Beyond

- Use the dedicated Attendant account model from Epic 3 as the baseline for all Epic 4 gate-operation stories.
- Keep Epic 4 stories explicit about which enforcement is now available because of Epic 3 configuration data and which parts still depend on later payment or contract workflows.
- Plan Epic 8 lease stories with the knowledge that they are now enabling natural Operator UAT, not just financial administration.
- Keep Epic 9 on the roadmap as a required normalization step before thesis-demo polishing. Epic 3 proved the shell debt is affecting workflow truth, not just aesthetics.

## Summary

Epic 3 succeeded in building the operational setup layer for the supply side of the parking system. The strongest outcome was architectural continuity: leased-lot access, versioned lot configuration, lot-wide pricing rules, and dedicated Attendant credentials all fit together cleanly. The biggest lesson was not about CRUD complexity; it was about reachability and lifecycle integrity. By the end of the epic, the team had working Operator features, but also clear proof that shell navigation, inactive-account enforcement, and real lease onboarding are part of the system’s practical completeness. Epic 4 can now move forward on a better foundation, while Epic 8 and Epic 9 have become sharper preparation priorities rather than background backlog items.
