## Context

- Epic 4 is not fully closed in sprint tracking: Stories 4.1 through 4.4 are `done`, while Stories 4.5 and 4.6 are currently `review`.
- This retrospective was run anyway at the Project Lead's request so the team can capture system-level learning from the first complete parking-loop slice before Epic 5 starts.
- Epic 4 covers the operational heart of the product: driver QR state, attendant gate entry, walk-in capture, attendant checkout preview, and swipe-based settlement.
- Previous retrospective commitments from Epic 3 were used as the comparison baseline for follow-through.

## Part 1: Epic Review

### What Went Well

- Epic 4 established the first coherent end-to-end parking loop instead of isolated features. By the end of the epic, the repo supports backend-issued driver check-in and check-out QR contracts, attendant-owned session creation, walk-in camera-first entry, attendant checkout preview, and payment-resolution settlement with bounded undo.
- The team preserved strong business boundaries across stories. Story 4.1 stayed pre-session only, Story 4.2 became the first session-creation boundary, Story 4.5 stayed read-only for fee preview, and Story 4.6 owned the only settlement mutation path.
- Reuse discipline remained strong. The same `sessions.py` boundary absorbed all Epic 4 contracts, and the same attendant gate screen expanded incrementally instead of fragmenting into separate operational shells.
- Verification quality improved as the epic progressed. Story 4.4, Story 4.5, and Story 4.6 all closed with full backend plus Flutter regression suites rather than only focused tests.
- Review added real value, especially late in the epic. Story 4.4 surfaced state-machine issues, and Story 4.6 surfaced settlement concurrency and stale-proof integrity concerns that were then patched before retrospective.

### What Did Not Go Well

- Epic 4 confirmed that “workflow implemented” and “epic closed” are not the same thing. At retrospective time, the two checkout stories are still in `review`, which means the operational loop is functionally present but not yet fully closed from a process standpoint.
- Checkout media scope is still asymmetric. Driver-app exit is well defined, but walk-in exit media and NFC-based checkout are still deliberately deferred, so the MVP loop is QR-first rather than genuinely universal yet.
- The shared attendant workspace became both a strength and a source of coupling. Adding walk-in, preview, settlement, and undo into one screen worked, but each new mode increased state-management complexity and regression surface.
- Transaction integrity issues were easy to miss on the first implementation pass. The most important problems found late were not syntax or CRUD issues; they were concurrent-settlement and stale-proof integrity risks that only appear when thinking about production behavior.
- Generated runtime artifacts from local/test flows still leaked into the repo surface, which shows the current test and upload hygiene is not fully sealed.

### Follow-Through From Epic 3 Retrospective

- Inactive-account enforcement as a platform rule: partially carried forward. Epic 4 stories assumed dedicated Attendant identities correctly, but the epic itself did not materially expand auth-lifecycle enforcement beyond depending on the Epic 3 baseline.
- Workflow reachability as part of story readiness: improved. Epic 4 features were reachable through the current app flows, especially on the attendant side, instead of remaining backend-only capabilities.
- Public multi-capability shell visibility: still unresolved. Driver-facing QR and active-session flows worked, but Epic 4 did not reduce the wider shell debt already identified in earlier retrospectives.
- Review as workflow-truth validation: strongly improved. The highest-value Epic 4 fixes came from checking race conditions, state transitions, and handoff boundaries rather than only happy-path feature presence.
- Keeping bridge solutions explicit: improved. The epic documents clearly distinguish current QR-first MVP behavior from future NFC and walk-in exit extensions instead of blending them into one ambiguous scope.

### Recurring Patterns Across Stories

- The best results came from keeping backend ownership of session truth, pricing truth, and QR state. Whenever the client stayed thin and the server remained authoritative, the story boundaries stayed understandable.
- State machines became the defining complexity of the epic. Driver idle vs active session, attendant check-in vs check-out mode, preview vs settlement, and checked-in vs checked-out state all needed explicit modeling.
- Review findings got more valuable as the stories moved from entry into exit. Early stories were mostly about workflow assembly; later stories were about transactional correctness, duplicate prevention, and no-look operational safety.
- The single attendant gate screen is viable, but every added mode now demands more intentional state-reset rules and stronger regression coverage.

### Root Lessons Learned

1. Epic 4 only worked because the team treated the parking loop as a sequence of state boundaries rather than one giant check-in/out feature.
2. Read-only preview and mutation settlement must remain separate contracts. Combining them would have made pricing trust, payment creation, and rollback behavior much harder to reason about.
3. Concurrency and stale-state review needs to happen before a story is considered genuinely stable in operational flows that affect money or occupancy.
4. Reusing one gate workspace is still the right choice, but it now requires explicit mode-reset discipline and higher regression investment to stay maintainable.
5. MVP scope control was a success. The team resisted inventing premature NFC checkout or walk-in exit credentials without product or contract backing.

## Part 2: Next Epic Preparation

### Next Epic Readiness

- Epic 5 can begin on a much stronger domain base because Epic 4 now provides authoritative session lifecycle data, payment linkage, and driver history-worthy completed sessions.
- The system is not fully closure-ready for Epic 4 until Stories 4.5 and 4.6 clear review and move to `done`, but the main domain contracts needed by later user-facing features are now present.
- The next preparation priority is less about missing core parking-loop capability and more about closure discipline, shell normalization, and future-proofing operational media extensions.

### Critical Discoveries That Affect Upcoming Epics

1. Epic 5 history and lot-detail work can rely on a real completed-session lifecycle now that Story 4.6 records payments and closes sessions explicitly.
2. Any future NFC or walk-in exit story must plug into the same checkout-preview and settlement contracts rather than inventing a second fee-calculation or session-closing path.
3. Payment-adjacent stories require concurrency thinking early. Transaction correctness cannot be treated as a final review concern once money and availability mutate together.
4. The current attendant workspace is good enough for MVP operation, but Epic 9 shell work remains important because operational screens are accumulating state-heavy behavior quickly.

### Team Agreements For Future Epics

- Do not merge checkout preview and settlement into one endpoint or one story boundary.
- Keep backend ownership of pricing, session state, and payment truth whenever financial or occupancy state changes.
- Treat review findings about race conditions and stale state as release blockers, not post-merge refinements.
- Keep story artifacts, sprint tracking, and review hardening notes synchronized in the same closure pass.

### Preparation Recommendations For Epic 5 and Beyond

- Start Epic 5 assuming completed parking sessions are now a first-class domain output, but do not mark Epic 4 fully closed until the remaining review states are resolved.
- Reuse the explicit Epic 4 checkout convention in future planning documents so later stories do not reopen payment-boundary ambiguity.
- Keep mobile operational screens regression-heavy. The shared attendant gate surface is now one of the highest state-density parts of the app.
- Treat Epic 9 shell work as risk reduction for future expansion, not just UI cleanup.

## Summary

Epic 4 delivered the first credible core parking loop for the project. The strongest success was boundary discipline: the team kept QR issuance, session creation, checkout preview, and settlement as separate server-owned contracts, which made the system easier to validate and harden. The biggest lesson was that operational correctness lives in state transitions and concurrency behavior, not just in happy-path feature completion. Even though Epic 4 is not fully closed in tracker terms yet, it already established the core domain architecture that later discovery, history, and advanced session-management epics can build on safely.
